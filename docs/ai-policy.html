AI use policy

Local Ed AI by DittoEd · Last updated July 2026

The short version: a general-purpose open-weight language model runs on your machine and drafts text from what you give it. It is a drafting assistant. It does not decide anything, it is not trained on your work, and everything it produces needs a human to read it before it goes anywhere.

What model runs, and where

Local Ed AI runs an open-weight language model locally through Ollama. The default is Phi-4 Mini (3.8B parameters, quantized), chosen because it runs at usable speed on ordinary school-issued laptops without a dedicated graphics card. You can select a different model during setup or later.

The model file lives on your machine and runs on your CPU. There is no API key, no hosted inference, and no vendor on the other end of the connection.

How your input is used

Your prompts are sent to the local model, which generates a response. That is the entire lifecycle. Specifically:

  • Your input is not used for training. The model weights are fixed and read-only. Nothing you type modifies them, and nothing is sent anywhere to train anything else.
  • Your input is not reviewed by a human. There is no maintainer-side review queue, because there is no maintainer-side anything.
  • Your input is not retained by any third party. It never reaches one.

What the application will not do

These are product boundaries, not just guidance:

  • It does not make eligibility determinations under IDEA or Section 504.
  • It does not make placement, services, or disciplinary determinations.
  • It does not evaluate, score, or grade a specific student.
  • It does not present itself as legal, medical, or psychological advice.

IEP drafts are watermarked as requiring full IEP team review. That watermark is deliberate and should not be removed before the draft is reviewed.

Known limitations you should plan around

  • It can be confidently wrong. Language models generate plausible text, not verified fact. A 3.8B model running on a laptop is meaningfully less capable than a large hosted model, and that tradeoff is the price of the privacy guarantee. Check anything factual.
  • It does not know your student. It only knows what you typed into that conversation. A draft that reads well may still be wrong for the child it is about.
  • It can reflect bias present in its training data. Pay particular attention to language describing student behavior, ability, and family circumstances, which is exactly where biased phrasing does the most harm in a permanent record.
  • It is not a compliance check. It does not verify that an IEP meets federal or Maryland requirements. Your district's compliance process still applies in full.

Recommended practice

  • Use it to get past the blank page, then edit in your own professional voice.
  • Read every generated line before it enters a student record or goes to a family.
  • Even though data never leaves your machine, use the minimum identifying detail necessary. Good habit, and it keeps drafts reusable.
  • Follow your district's AI policy. This tool is designed to make compliance easier, not to substitute for it.

For districts writing AI policy

Local Ed AI is intended to be a straightforward case under most district AI policies and under the Maryland State Department of Education's AI guidance: the model is open-weight, inference is local, no student data is transmitted or retained by any vendor, the source is auditable, and every output is human-reviewed by design. Districts are welcome to reference this page directly in their tool inventories and vetting documentation.
